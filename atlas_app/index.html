Cities are complex systems of spatial relationships (Batty, 2012, 2013). Once space is understood as a complex interconnected system it is clear that certain areas, or nodes, are more centrally located than others. Centrality is operationalized by means of various indexes that capture certain aspect of it. Several centrality indices have been developed since Bavelas first introduced the concept to human communication studies (Crucitti, Latora, & Porta, 2006) reach and betweenness are just a few examples of these.

Recently it has been argued that there is a strong relationship between centrality and crime patterns (Kim & Choi, 2016; Summers & Johnson, 2016). Centrality indexes are good predictors of pedestrian movement (Hillier & Hanson, 1989); more people passing through could also mean an increase of suitable targets according to RAT.

Is there a correlation between assaults and streets’ centrality in Denver?

Has been there any change on this pattern from 2012 to 2016?

Besides the motivation provided by these questions, the idea of this ATLAS, or map application, is to explore data driven visualizations in the context of maps. Therefore, the more technical questions could be stated as follows:

How feasible would it be to create an ATLAS using esri’s cedar.js & esri’s ArcGIS API for JavaScript 3.20?

What are the best practices for dealing with big datasets using JavaScript?

The number of Assaults per year was derived from City of Denver Open Catalog's crime point dataset which contains 414, 674 crime occurrences of various kinds from 2012 – 2017. Python was utilized to derive new fields selecting only the crimes categorized under assault. The street network was obtained from Open Street Maps by using OSMnx. Following, the relevant centrality scores, Reach, Betweenness & Closeness,for each street segment (over 27,000) were calculated using MIT’s Urban Network Analysis Toolbox. Next, a spatial join was performed in order to relate both datasets (assaults –points- & street network with centrality scores). Finally, rank fields were calculated for every centrality score using the natural breaks algorithm, the data was cleaned and grouped and new shapefiles were produced. The final shapefiles were then published to ArcGIS Online in order to be consumed for the application. Simple Spearman's rho correlations were performed on the final relevant fields.

The streets are color coded from lowest (blue) to highest (red) number of assaults. The street segment with the higher number of assaults overall was 14th street, between Glenarm Place & Tremont Place.
The relationship between centrality scores per street segment (independent variable X) & number of assaults per street segment (dependent variable Y) was analyzed using Spearman's & Pearson's correlation coefficients.
Spearman’s rho: Closeness (-0.092293) | Reach (0.081950) | Betweenness (0.023815)
Pearson’s: Closeness (-0.004599) | Reach (0.067997) | Betweenness (0.009385)
No significant linear or monotonic relationships between the centrality scores and number of assaults per street was found. Other statistical analysis would be required in order to investigate other kinds of relationships.

I took a minimalistic approach to the design of the application; the text was kept at minimum, transparent pop ups were used, no legends, no street labels, and no ticks or axes lines for the scatter plots. The color palette is muted; bright colors are only used to emphasize the patterns in the data. The main reasons behind this design are:
A very flexible medium, not everything should be visible all the time; even though street labels were not used, the user can click a street segment to discover relevant information such as the street name.
Data driven graphics,the map is the window that drives the data in the scatterplots; this allows exploring data patterns at different scales at a click of a button.
The audience, the people who are going to be interacting with the application are very familiar with maps, the area and the medium.
